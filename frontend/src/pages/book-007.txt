Thinking, Fast and Slow
by Daniel Kahneman

System 1 operates automatically and quickly.
System 2 allocates attention to effortful mental activities.

Daniel Kahneman spent decades studying the two systems of thought that govern human decision-making. His findings upended assumptions about human rationality and won him the Nobel Prize.

System 1: fast, intuitive, emotional. It recognizes faces. It completes the phrase "bread and..." without effort. It drives on empty roads. It jumps to conclusions.

System 2: slow, deliberate, logical. It fills out tax forms. It parks in a tight space. It monitors behavior in social situations. It is lazy when it can be.

The problem: we often use System 1 when we should use System 2.

Cognitive biases are not errors of calculation — they are features of a system that evolved for speed and survival, not for accuracy.

Anchoring. Availability heuristic. The planning fallacy. Overconfidence. Loss aversion.

Kahneman does not promise to cure bias. He promises something more useful: awareness.

"Nothing in life is as important as you think it is while you are thinking about it."

Knowing about biases does not eliminate them. But it helps you notice when you are most vulnerable.
