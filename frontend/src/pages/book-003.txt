Dune
by Frank Herbert

A beginning is the time for taking the most delicate care.

Arrakis. The desert planet. The only source of the spice melange — the most valuable substance in the known universe. Melange extends life. Melange expands consciousness. Melange makes interstellar travel possible.

Paul Atreides is fifteen years old when his family is assigned to govern Arrakis. He has trained his whole life for this. But nothing prepares him for the Fremen — the desert people who have survived the harshest environment in the galaxy.

The dunes stretch beyond the horizon. Heat shimmers against rock. Below the sand, something massive moves — a sandworm, three hundred meters long, riding pressure differentials like a swimmer cuts water.

The spice has a smell: cinnamon and something else. Something ancient.

Paul closes his eyes. He has seen this moment before, in his dreams. He has seen what he might become. He is not sure he wants it.

"The mystery of life is not a problem to be solved but a reality to be experienced."

The desert teaches patience. The desert teaches truth.

Arrakis is waiting.
