The Great Gatsby
by F. Scott Fitzgerald

In my younger and more vulnerable years my father gave me some advice.

"Whenever you feel like criticizing anyone," he told me, "just remember that all the people in this world haven't had the advantages that you've had."

Across the bay, a single green light blinks at the end of a dock. Jay Gatsby stares at it every night from his mansion on West Egg. He throws parties for hundreds of people who barely know his name. He has reinvented himself from nothing. He believes absolutely in the green light at the end of Daisy's dock.

Nick Carraway narrates the summer of 1922 — a summer of parties and bootleg liquor and impossible dreams. Gatsby's shirts cascade in rainbow arcs. His smile suggests that he has seen you clearly and found you good.

But beneath the shimmer of Long Island Sound lies something colder.

The Valley of Ashes. The eyes of Doctor T.J. Eckleburg watching from a faded billboard. The restless energy of people who have confused motion with progress.

"So we beat on, boats against the current, borne back ceaselessly into the past."

The green light burns on.
