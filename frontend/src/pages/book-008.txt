1984
by George Orwell

It was a bright cold day in April, and the clocks were striking thirteen.

Winston Smith works at the Ministry of Truth, where his job is to rewrite history — to make the past conform to the Party's current version of reality. He is a small, thin man of thirty-nine with a varicose ulcer above his right ankle.

Big Brother is watching.

Telescreen in every room. Thought Police in every shadow. The language itself is being simplified — Newspeak — until the words to express dissent no longer exist.

Doublethink: the ability to hold two contradictory beliefs simultaneously and accept both of them. WAR IS PEACE. FREEDOM IS SLAVERY. IGNORANCE IS STRENGTH.

Winston begins a diary. This is already a capital offense.

He falls in love with Julia. This too is dangerous.

He meets a man named O'Brien and believes he is part of the Brotherhood — a secret resistance. He is wrong.

The Party does not merely want obedience. It wants the love of those it controls.

"If you want a picture of the future, imagine a boot stamping on a human face — forever."

Winston holds onto one thought: they cannot get inside him. They cannot make him stop loving Julia.

He is wrong about that too.
